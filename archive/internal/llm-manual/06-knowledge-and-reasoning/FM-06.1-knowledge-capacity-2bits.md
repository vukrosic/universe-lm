# FM-06.1 — A model stores ~2 bits of knowledge per parameter

**Rule.** A sufficiently-trained transformer stores at most **~2 bits of factual knowledge per
parameter** — a *capacity ceiling*, roughly constant across sizes and architectures, and
holding even at **int8** quantization. Implication: a 7B model can hold ~14 Gbit ≈ 1.7 GB of
facts (more than English Wikipedia + textbooks, by their estimate).

**Confidence.** **[F] Frontier.** One exceptionally rigorous program (Allen-Zhu & Li, ICLR
2025), internally replicated across many synthetic datasets and sizes, but not yet
independently reproduced at scale by other labs on other setups. (Independent replication on a
different synthetic protocol would upgrade it toward [C]/[E].)

**Scope.** Allen-Zhu & Li 2024, "Physics of LM Part 3.3." Synthetic factual tuples (e.g.
(person, attribute, value)) with *known* bit content; transformers with ≥2 layers; "sufficient
training" (enough exposures — see caveat). "Knowledge" = retrievable factual tuples, **not**
reasoning or skills.

## Evidence / details

- Synthetic data lets them compute information content exactly: a birthday drawn from 12×28×200
  options carries `log₂(12·28·200) ≈ 60.2` bits. They then measure how many such bits a trained
  model can recover → bits-per-parameter.
- **~2 bit/param** holds across a wide size range and data types, and **survives int8**
  quantization (drops only at int4). So the ceiling is about *representational capacity*, not
  precision waste.
- The paper reports **12 results** on how capacity responds to: training duration, architecture,
  quantization, **MoE sparsity** (MoE keeps most of the capacity despite sparse activation), and
  **data signal-to-noise ratio** (junk/diverse-source data can *lower* the constant — prepending
  a source tag recovers it).

## Caveats / where the 2 moves

- **Exposure matters — and roughly halves it.** The 2-bit ceiling needs each fact seen *enough
  times*. The paper's widely-cited training-duration result: ~2 bit/param in the well-trained
  regime (each fact seen ≈1000×) drops to **~1 bit/param** when knowledge-starved (≈100×). At
  ~100 exposures, architecture also starts to matter (it doesn't at 1000×). *Caveat on this
  caveat:* the exact 1000×/100× → 2/1 numbers are the paper's own, reported widely but **not
  re-verified by us against the paper's tables this pass** — treat the halving as solid, the
  precise exposure thresholds as the paper's claim. (Ties to FM-06.2 and FM-01.2.)
- **Junk data is expensive.** Mixing low-quality "junk" sources can degrade capacity for the
  good data by a large factor (~20× reported, in the under-trained regime); **prepending a
  source/domain token largely recovers it** — a concrete data-engineering lever.
- **It's a ceiling, not a typical.** Real corpora with noisy/rare facts realize *less* than 2
  bit/param; the number is the best case under clean, sufficiently-repeated data.
- The constant is for *stored facts*, and says nothing about reasoning/generalization capacity.

## Why it's load-bearing for a builder

This is the rule that turns "how big a model do I need?" into arithmetic. If your application
needs to *memorize* `K` bits of facts, you need ≳ `K/2` parameters' worth of capacity for that
knowledge alone — independent of, and on top of, whatever capacity reasoning needs. It also
reframes MoE (cheap capacity for knowledge) and quantization (int8 ≈ free, int4 ≈ lossy) in
capacity terms.

## Reproduced by us?

**No — out of our scale and data regime.** Our 1–3M-param runs on real text can't cleanly
measure bits-per-param (real text has unknown bit content). But the *method* is directly
borrowable: a synthetic-tuple probe at tiny scale could test whether the 2-bit constant holds
or bends at 1–3M params. Genuinely novel if it does — a possible ledger spin-off, though it
breaks our "real text, structural levers" frame, so flag before pursuing.

## Falsifier

A sufficiently-trained, in-scope model that stores materially more than ~2 bit/param (clean
synthetic facts, int8) — or a clean failure to reach ~2 even with ample exposures — would
break or bound the constant.
