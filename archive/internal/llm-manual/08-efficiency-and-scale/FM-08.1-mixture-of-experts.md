# FM-08.1 — MoE buys capacity at near-constant compute; many small experts win

**Rule.** A Mixture-of-Experts FFN — many expert sub-networks, only a few activated per token
by a router — lets you grow *total* parameters (capacity) while holding *active* parameters
(per-token compute) nearly fixed. Two design choices that consistently help: **fine-grained
experts** (more, smaller experts beat few large ones) and **a few always-on shared experts**.

**Confidence.** **[C] Contested/Established-trending.** MoE is load-bearing in shipped frontier
models (DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral, Qwen-MoE, Gemini-class) — strong evidence it works. The
*optimal* configuration (granularity, expert count, shared ratio, active fraction) is an active
research area, hence [C] rather than [E].

**Scope.** Decoder LLMs; the granularity scaling law was fit by Krajewski et al. 2024 across a
range of compute budgets. Benefits grow with scale — MoE's edge over dense *increases* with
compute, and is smaller (or negative, due to overhead) at tiny scale.

## Evidence / details

- **Capacity vs compute decoupling.** DeepSeek-V3: **671B total params, 37B active per token**
  — ~18× more capacity than it pays for per token. This is the whole point of MoE.
- **Fine-grained experts win.** Krajewski et al. 2024 ("Scaling Laws for Fine-Grained MoE")
  introduce **granularity G** (how finely each expert is subdivided vs a standard FFN) and find
  the common practice of sizing experts = the FFN is **suboptimal at almost any compute
  budget**; optimal G > 1 (many small experts). MoE's efficiency advantage over dense grows
  with model size/compute.
- **Shared + fine-grained routing.** DeepSeekMoE: split experts into finer segments *and*
  isolate a few **shared experts** for common knowledge (reduces redundancy among routed
  experts). Their 16B matched LLaMA-2-7B at ~40% of the compute.
- **Knowledge capacity survives sparsity.** FM-06.1: MoE retains most of the ~2 bit/param
  knowledge capacity despite activating few experts — so MoE is genuinely "cheap capacity for
  facts," not just cheap FLOPs.
- **Costs:** routing adds complexity, load-balancing/auxiliary losses, all-to-all communication,
  and total memory footprint (all experts must be stored even if few activate).

## Why it's load-bearing for a builder

If you need a lot of knowledge capacity (FM-06.1) but can't afford the inference cost of a dense
model that big, MoE is the lever. For a compute-constrained team it cuts *training and serving*
FLOPs for a given capability — at the price of memory and engineering complexity. Not worth it
for a small first model; essential at the frontier.

## Reproduced by us?

**No — out of scope.** MoE's advantage grows with scale and can be net-negative at our 1–3M
regime (routing overhead dominates). The only thread to us is the FM-06.1 capacity framing.

## Falsifier

The granularity rule is bounded if, in-scope, expert-size = FFN-size proves compute-optimal
(no benefit from G > 1). The MoE rule overall is bounded if dense matches MoE at equal *total*
compute including routing/comm overhead at frontier scale.
