# FM-01.1 — Compute-optimal training scales N and D together (~20 tokens/param)

**Rule.** For a fixed compute budget `C ≈ 6·N·D`, validation loss is minimized by scaling
model size `N` and training tokens `D` *together* — roughly **20 training tokens per
parameter**. Doubling the budget means doubling *both* N and D, not just N.

**Confidence.** **[E] Established.** The single most-replicated quantitative law in LLM
training. Independently reproduced (Cerebras-GPT, 111M–13B) and a careful re-analysis (Epoch
AI) confirmed the parametric law while tightening the estimates.

**Scope.** Fit on dense decoder transformers, **44M–16B params, 5B–500B tokens**, standard
web-text distribution, single epoch, cosine LR schedule tuned to the run length. Outside
this — heavily curated/distilled data, reasoning-specialized models, or
**inference-cost-aware** budgets — the optimal ratio shifts (see caveats).

## Evidence

Hoffmann et al. 2022 ("Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models", the *Chinchilla*
paper) used three independent methods that agreed:

1. **Fixed model / vary tokens.**
2. **IsoFLOP** — hold `C` fixed, sweep the N↔D split, find the minimum.
3. **Parametric fit** of `L(N,D) = E + A/N^α + B/D^β`; the fitted exponents `α ≈ β`, which
   is *why* the optimal split keeps N and D in near-lockstep.

The proof-of-law: **Chinchilla (70B params, 1.4T tokens)** beat **Gopher (280B, ~300B
tokens)** at *equal compute* — 67.5% vs 60% MMLU — by being 4× smaller and trained on ~4.7×
more data. Ratio ≈ 20 tokens/param.

The retroactive lesson: most prior "large" models were **badly undertrained**. GPT-3 (175B)
used ~2 tokens/param; the Chinchilla rule says it wanted ~3.5T tokens (20×175B), ~10× what it
got. *Parameters were cheap glory; tokens were the bottleneck.*

### What Chinchilla corrected: the Kaplan law

This rule *overturned* the earlier **Kaplan et al. 2020** scaling laws, which concluded the
opposite — "train very large models on a relatively modest amount of data, and stop well
before convergence" (i.e. scale parameters *faster* than data). Acting on Kaplan is what
produced the undertrained GPT-3 generation. **Why they disagreed** (verified, Pearce & Song
2024, "Reconciling Kaplan and Chinchilla Scaling Laws"): the dominant cause is that **Kaplan
counted *non-embedding* parameters and fit at *small scale***; simulating Chinchilla's study
under those two choices reproduces Kaplan-like (biased) coefficients. *Note:* a popular
alternative story blames Kaplan's fixed-length cosine LR schedule (not decayed to match each
run) — plausible and often repeated, but Pearce & Song find parameter-counting + scale, not
the schedule, is the primary driver. Cited here so we don't propagate the unverified version.

## Caveats / where the 20 moves

- **Inference cost flips the calculus.** Chinchilla minimizes *training* compute. If a model
  will serve many tokens, it is rational to *overtrain a smaller model* (more tokens/param)
  to cut lifetime inference cost — why production models (Llama family) sit at 100s–1000s of
  tokens/param, far past 20. The 20:1 is the training-optimum, not the deployment-optimum.
  Made rigorous by **Sardana et al. 2024 ("Beyond Chinchilla-Optimal")**: accounting for
  ~1B expected inference requests, the compute-optimal choice shifts to *smaller, longer-
  trained* models, with quality still improving at token/param ratios up to **10,000:1** (47
  models). This is the single most important real-world correction to the 20:1 rule.
- **Replication nuances.** Epoch AI's replication found the original confidence intervals
  were wide and the three methods disagreed somewhat; their refit still lands near 20 but
  with caveats. Some model families empirically sit at 10–12 tokens/param.
- **Task-dependence.** Roberts et al. 2025: *memorization* benefits from more params (lower
  tokens/param); *reasoning* benefits from more data (higher tokens/param). One ratio does
  not fit all skills.

## Reproduced by us?

**Not by us, and not reproducible at our scale.** Our ledger lives at 1–3M params / 3M tokens
(≈1 token/param — deeply data-bound by choice, to isolate structural levers). We **cite**
this law; we do not own it. The relevant open question for us is the *opposite* direction:
which **structural** findings at our tiny, token-starved regime transfer up to the
Chinchilla-regime models this law governs (ledger agenda **G3**).

## Falsifier

A clean IsoFLOP sweep, in-scope (dense transformer, single-epoch web text, schedule tuned to
length), whose loss minimum sits far from `D/N ≈ 20` (say <8 or >40) would bound or break the
rule. Inference-aware or curated-data regimes do **not** falsify it — they are out of scope.
