# FM-01.3 — Larger models deserve larger vocabularies

**Rule.** The compute-optimal vocabulary size grows (as a power law) with model size. Most
shipped LLMs use vocabularies that are **too small** for their parameter count; sizing the
vocabulary up toward the optimum improves loss and downstream accuracy at equal FLOPs.

**Confidence.** **[C] Contested.** One strong, validated paper establishes the direction and
a predictive law; a follow-up shows the benefit reverses past a threshold, and production
practice varies widely (32K–256K). Direction is solid; the *exact* optimum is regime-bound.

**Scope.** Tao et al. 2024, models **33M–3B params**, up to 500B characters, BPE tokenizers.
Optimum depends jointly on non-vocab params *and* the data budget (rare-token undertraining
bites in the data-constrained regime).

## Evidence

Tao et al. 2024, "Scaling Laws with Vocabulary: Larger Models Deserve Larger Vocabularies" —
three methods (IsoFLOP, derivative, parametric fit) agree that optimal vocab size follows a
power law in non-vocabulary parameters.

- Headline: **Llama-2-70B's 32K vocab should have been ≥216K** (~7× larger) to be
  compute-optimal.
- Validation: at fixed 2.3e21 FLOPs, raising vocab 32K→43K improved ARC-Challenge 29.1→32.0.

The two competing forces that create an interior optimum:
- **Bigger vocab helps:** fewer tokens per sentence (better fertility) + more
  representational capacity.
- **Bigger vocab hurts:** rare tokens get few gradient updates → undertrained embeddings,
  worse in data-limited settings; and decode-time / embedding-table cost rises.

**Counter-evidence (the [C]):** Huang et al. 2025, "Scaling Embedding Layers in Language
Models" — past a threshold, larger vocabularies *degrade* BPC and inflate decode cost;
benefit is not monotonic.

## Practical notes

- **Compare models with BPC (bits-per-character), not cross-entropy loss.** CE is
  vocab-sensitive — larger vocabularies mechanically show higher CE — so CE cannot compare
  tokenizers fairly. This is a frequent benchmarking error.
- Production already pushes this: MiniCPM uses ~122K vocab on a 2.4B model.

## Reproduced by us?

**Not yet — and tractable for us.** Vocabulary size is a clean, cheap lever at 1–3M params.
A controlled BPC-vs-vocab sweep at our scale would test whether "larger models deserve
larger vocabularies" inverts at tiny scale (plausibly *smaller* models deserve *smaller*
vocab — the rare-token-starvation force dominates when both params and data are tiny). Good
candidate for a ledger draft.

## Falsifier

An in-scope IsoFLOP sweep where loss/BPC is flat or *decreasing* in vocab size across the
range a model can afford (no interior optimum, no power-law in N) would break the rule.
