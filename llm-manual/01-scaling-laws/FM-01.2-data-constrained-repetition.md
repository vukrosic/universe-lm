# FM-01.2 — Under a data ceiling, repeating data up to ~4 epochs is ~free

**Rule.** When you have less unique text than the compute-optimum wants, repeating the corpus
is nearly as good as fresh data **up to ~4 epochs** (loss penalty negligible). Returns then
decay: meaningful gains persist to ~16 epochs and effectively vanish by ~40. When
data-bound, prefer a **smaller model trained for more epochs** over a bigger one.

**Confidence.** **[E] Established** for the ~4-epoch result; the 16/40 decay points are from
a single (strong, large) study and best read as **[C]**.

**Scope.** Muennighoff et al. 2023, up to **9B params and 900B training tokens**, standard
web text, repeating a fixed monolithic corpus. The "value of repetition" is corpus- and
quality-dependent; cleaner/more-diverse corpora tolerate more repetition than noisy ones.

## Evidence

Muennighoff et al. 2023, "Scaling Data-Constrained Language Models" — extends Chinchilla
(FM-01.1) to the case where unique tokens run out, with an explicit decay term for repeated
tokens' diminishing value:

| Repetition | Effect on loss vs equal fresh tokens |
|---|---|
| up to ~4 epochs | negligible degradation ("free") |
| up to ~16 epochs | still meaningful gains |
| ~40 epochs | returns ≈ 0 |

The counterintuitive allocation result: when data-constrained, **scale epochs faster than
parameters.** Their best data-constrained model beat the Chinchilla-prescribed allocation
with **27% fewer parameters** by training longer on repeated data.

Why repetition eventually hurts connects to FM-02.2 (dedup): repeated tokens behave more and
more like memorization pressure as epochs and model size grow, so the marginal token teaches
less.

## Caveats

- The 4/16/40 numbers are *averages over a regime*; the knee depends on corpus quality and
  diversity. A small, pristine corpus repeats better than a large, dirty one.
- Repetition interacts with weight decay and LR schedule; these were held at sensible
  defaults, not co-optimized per epoch-count.

## Reproduced by us?

**Partially adjacent.** Our tiny runs are deliberately *data-constrained* (3M tokens) and
already lean on repetition, but we have not run a controlled epoch-count sweep to locate our
own knee. **Open question worth a draft:** does the ~4-epoch "free" zone hold at 1–3M
params, or does the knee move at tiny scale? This is cheap for us to measure and would be a
genuine ledger contribution.

## Falsifier

An in-scope run where the 4th epoch already costs ≥ the noise band relative to fresh tokens
(i.e. the "free" zone is shorter than claimed for standard web text) would bound the rule
downward.
